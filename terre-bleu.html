Planting Lavender

Getting Started with Lavender, Key Conditions for Success & How to Keep Your Lavender Thriving

Lavender Growing Guide

Tips and tricks for beautiful, fragrant blooms in your garden

Lavender's soothing scent and vibrant purple blooms make it a garden favoriteâ€”but growing it successfully isn't as challenging as you might think. With the right conditions and care, you can enjoy this Mediterranean beauty in your own backyard. Let's explore everything you need to know to plant and nurture lavender like a pro.

Finding the Perfect Spot for Lavender

Sunshine is absolutely critical for lavender success. Find the sunniest spot in your yard, as lavender thrives in full sunlight. This is the most important factor for encouraging strong, healthy growth and vibrant blooms.

Aim for at least six to eight hours of direct sunlight each day for the best results. It's a Mediterranean plant that loves warmth and doesn't mind a bit of wind.

Preparing the Right Soil

Lavender loves sandy loam, which is pretty common in Ontario. But if you're dealing with the heavy clay soil found along the lakeside of the Escarpment, the biggest challenge isn't the soil itselfâ€”it's drainage.

Critical Drainage Information:

Lavender absolutely cannot tolerate standing water. If it sits in a puddle for even a couple of days, it's done. A low spot in clay soil? That's the worst-case scenario.

In France, lavender thrives in rocky, calcium-rich fields alongside grapevines, and that can work here too. At Terre Bleu, some of our biggest, healthiest lavender plants grow alongside roses in well-nurtured soil topped with rich mulch from a local chicken farmer.

Soil Improvement Tips:

  • Add sand or fine gravel to improve drainage
  • Consider raised beds for areas with heavy clay soil
  • Aim for slightly alkaline soil (pH 6.5-7.5)
  • Use porous ground cover to prevent weeds

Planting at the Right Time

Timing matters when planting lavender:

Once the risk of frost has passed, it's time to plantâ€”but don't rush it! A prolonged cold snap can wipe out young lavender plants. Based on experience, we never plant before June 1st. By then, the soil has warmed to around 12Â°C, and we've already prepped the ground.

Step-by-Step Planting Instructions

  1. Prepare the soil with good drainage in mind
  2. Consider using ground cover - At Terre Bleu, we cover the soil with a porous nylon weave ground cover to save ourselves from endless weeding
  3. Handle roots with care - When transplanting, be gentle with the roots! Some gardeners rough them up when taking them out of the potâ€”we don't. Roots are everything.
  4. Water generously at first - Making sure your plants don't dry out for at least 30 to 60 days, depending on the weather
  5. Watch for signs of over/under watering - If the lower leaves start turning yellow, you might be overwateringâ€”cut back a little. If the plant starts to wilt, it likely needs more water.

Lavender Growth Cycle

Watch how lavender develops through its complete growth cycle:

EARLY SPRING

Seedling Stage

Lavender begins as a small seedling with just a few leaves. During this vulnerable stage, it needs consistent moisture and protection from extreme temperatures.

This is when the plant establishes its roots, which will be critical for its future health and drought resistance.

LATE SPRING

Vegetative Growth

The plant focuses on developing its foliage and structure. Lavender's signature silver-green leaves spread out to form a bushy shape.

This is the time to ensure good air circulation and proper spacing, as crowded plants may develop fungal issues. No fertilizer needed during this stage!

EARLY SUMMER

Bud Formation

As days lengthen and temperatures rise, lavender begins to send up flower stalks. The characteristic buds form at the tips of these stalks.

This is a perfect time to reduce watering, which encourages stronger fragrance development. The plant is now establishing its drought-tolerance.

MID SUMMER

Full Bloom

This is the glory stage! The flower stalks elongate and buds open to reveal vibrant purple blooms. The plant is at its most aromatic and attracts plenty of beneficial pollinators.

For the most fragrant harvests, pick flowers when they're about halfway open. Morning harvesting after the dew has dried yields the strongest essential oils.

LATE SUMMER

Seed Formation

After blooming, the plant focuses energy on producing seeds. The flowers begin to fade, and tiny seeds develop within the spent blooms.

This is the perfect time for pruning - remove about one-third of the current year's growth to maintain the plant's compact shape and encourage bushiness in the next growing season.

FALL & WINTER

Dormancy

Lavender enters its dormant period as temperatures drop. Growth slows dramatically, and the plant concentrates energy in its woody base and roots.

No need to fertilize during dormancy. Good drainage becomes even more critical during winter months - excess moisture during dormancy is the primary cause of lavender loss in cold climates.

Watering Wisely

Lavender is drought-tolerant once established, but needs careful attention early on:

  • First year: Water deeply about once per week (about 1 inch)
  • Established plants: Water only every two weeks or less if it rains
  • Warning signs: Yellow leaves indicate overwatering; wilting suggests underwatering

Watering Rule of Thumb:

It's better to underwater lavender than overwater it. When in doubt, wait another day.

Seasonal Care Timeline

Lavender needs different care throughout the year. Here's what to do each season:

Spring (March-May)
  • Clear away winter debris
  • Prune back any dead stems
  • Add a light gravel mulch around plants
  • Plant new lavender after danger of frost (around June 1 in Ontario)
  • Apply a light organic fertilizer if needed
Summer (June-August)
  • Harvest blooms when flowers are half-open
  • Water only during extended dry spells
  • Monitor for pests (though rarely a problem)
  • Enjoy peak blooming season!
Fall (September-November)
  • Main pruning time after flowering
  • Cut back by one-third
  • Remove spent flower stalks
  • Reduce watering as temperatures cool
  • Last chance to plant before winter
Winter (December-February)
  • Add gravel mulch to protect roots
  • Avoid overhead protection (lavender needs air)
  • No watering needed
  • Plan for next year's planting

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Poor Drainage Problems +

If your soil doesn't drain well, lavender roots will rot. Signs include yellowing leaves from the bottom up and a mushy stem base. The solution is to either transplant to a better-draining location, create a raised bed, or heavily amend your soil with sand and gravel. In extreme cases, you may need to start over with new plants in a better location.

Yellow Leaves +

Yellowing leaves usually indicate overwatering. Let the soil dry out completely between waterings. If the problem persists, check that your soil drains properly. Yellow leaves can occasionally signal nutrient deficiencies, but lavender typically thrives in poor soil, so this is less likely than water issues.

Few or No Blooms +

If your lavender is growing but not blooming well, check for these common issues: insufficient sunlight (less than 6 hours daily), improper pruning (too aggressive or too timid), excessive nitrogen fertilizer (promotes leaf growth at the expense of flowers), or improper timing (some varieties bloom later than others).

Woody, Leggy Growth +

Lavender naturally develops woody stems as it ages, but excessive woodiness and leggy growth usually indicate insufficient pruning. Regular pruning (removing about one-third of growth after flowering) keeps plants compact and encourages new growth. Never cut into old wood as it won't regenerate. If a plant becomes too woody, it's often best to replace it.

Lavender Varieties to Consider

Different lavender varieties offer unique characteristics. Here are some top choices for Ontario gardens:

English Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia) +

The most cold-hardy variety, perfect for Canadian gardens. Popular cultivars include 'Hidcote' (compact, deep purple), 'Munstead' (early bloomer), and 'Royal Velvet' (long stems, great for cutting). English lavender has the sweetest fragrance and is ideal for culinary use.

Lavandin (Lavandula x intermedia) +

A hybrid between English and Portuguese lavender, with exceptional vigor and large flower heads. Popular cultivars include 'Grosso' (high oil content), 'Provence' (classic French fields), and 'Phenomenal' (excellent cold hardiness). Great for dried bouquets and crafts.

French Lavender (Lavandula dentata) +

Distinguished by its toothed leaves, this variety is less cold-hardy but offers a longer blooming season. It works well in containers that can be moved indoors for winter protection. Its unique camphor-like scent makes it distinctive in the garden.

Spanish Lavender (Lavandula stoechas) +

Recognized by its distinctive "rabbit ear" bracts atop each flower spike. This showy variety blooms earlier than other types but is the least cold-hardy. Excellent for containers and southern gardens, or as an annual in colder regions.

Harvesting Your Lavender

For the most fragrant and vibrant blooms:

  • Harvest when flowers are about half open
  • Cut in the morning after dew has dried
  • Leave at least 2 inches of the current year's growth
  • Bundle stems together with twine for drying
  • Hang bundles upside down in a dark, dry place

Properly dried lavender will retain its scent and color for months!

Companion Planting with Lavender

Lavender plays well with many garden plants that share similar growing conditions. Here are some perfect companions:

  • Roses: Lavender helps repel aphids that often attack roses
  • Herbs: Thyme, sage, and rosemary share similar soil and water needs
  • Drought-tolerant perennials: Echinacea, yarrow, and sedum create beautiful combinations
  • Mediterranean plants: Olive trees, santolina, and artemisia make natural companions

Just remember to give each plant enough space for proper air circulation!
